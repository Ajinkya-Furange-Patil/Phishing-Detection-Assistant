## A Comprehensive Dataset and Engineering Blueprint

## Executive Summary

The landscape of cyber threat intelligence has undergone a foundational paradigm shift, driven by the escalating complexity of social engineering and technical evasion tactics. The historical reliance on monolithic, heuristic-based filtering algorithms is no longer mathematically or operationally sufficient to combat the multi-vector nature of contemporary phishing attacks. 

Threat actors now employ:
- Highly obfuscated payloads
- Algorithmically generated deceptive domains
- Meticulously crafted psychological triggers
- Synthetically generated text powered by Large Language Models (LLMs)

To effectively neutralize these threats, the structural architecture of detection systems must evolve from single-algorithm paradigms into highly specialized, multi-modular stacking ensembles.

## Introduction

This exhaustive research report dictates the precise data acquisition, feature engineering, and model training requirements for a **five-tier Stacking Ensemble architecture** designed to classify electronic communications as legitimate or malicious. 

### Five Analytical Modules:

1. **Content Analysis**
2. **Uniform Resource Locator (URL) Analysis**
3. **Header Verification**
4. **Attachment Diagnostics**
5. **Behavioral Profiling**

The individual probabilistic outputs of these five modules are subsequently aggregated and processed by an **XGBoost meta-learner** to render a final classification decision.

### Success Factors

The success of such a complex, heterogeneous architecture relies entirely on:
- **Quality, structure, and diversity** of the underlying training data
- **Progressive implementation** of the technology stack

This document provides:
- An exhaustive evaluation of the primary datasets necessary to train each independent module
- Details on the causal relationships between feature extraction techniques and model performance
- A practical blueprint for implementing this architecture within a comprehensive Python-based ecosystem

## The Architectural Paradigm: Stacking Ensembles in Cyber Defense

Before exploring the individual datasets required for this undertaking, it is necessary to establish the operational mechanics of the proposed Stacking Ensemble. 

### Design Philosophy

The architecture deliberately avoids forcing a single algorithm to map disparate data types—such as raw text, IP addresses, binary entropy, and authentication headers—into a single latent space. Instead, it deploys **specialized algorithms** tailored precisely to the mathematical structure of specific data domains.

### Email Processing Pipeline

The final architecture models the flow of an email through **five parallel extraction and classification pipelines**:

| Architectural Tier | Analytical Focus | Required Inputs | Algorithmic Approach | Target Output |
|-------------------|------------------|-----------------|---------------------|---------------|
| **Module 1** | Email Content Analysis | Raw Email Text | NLP Transformers (BERT, DistilBERT) | `bert_score` (e.g., 0.91) |
| **Module 2** | URL Analysis | Embedded Links | Logistic Regression, Random Forest | `url_score` (e.g., 0.84) |
| **Module 3** | Header Analysis | Routing & Auth Headers | Random Forest | `header_score` (e.g., 0.95) |
| **Module 4** | Attachment Analysis | File Metadata | XGBoost | `attachment_score` (e.g., 0.76) |
| **Module 5** | Behavioral Features | Psycholinguistic Markers | NLP / Rule-Based Heuristics | `behavior_score` (e.g., 0.88) |
| **Feature Fusion** | Output Aggregation | 5-Dimensional Array | Array Construction | `[0.91, 0.84, 0.95, 0.76, 0.88]` |
| **Meta-Learner** | Final Classification | Fused Probability Array | XGBoost | Final Probability (e.g., 98.2% Phishing) |

### Key Concept

The independent probabilities generated by the initial five modules serve as the **inputs—or engineered features—for the Feature Fusion Layer**. This layer constructs an entirely new tabular dataset composed strictly of model predictions, which subsequently trains the XGBoost Meta-Learner. 

The ensuing sections detail the specific datasets and feature extraction techniques required to facilitate this highly specific training pipeline.

## Module 1: Email Content Analysis

### Objective

The objective of the first module is to extract **meaning, context, and intent** from the unstructured raw text of the email body. 

### Designated Models

**BERT** (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) and its computationally lighter variant, **DistilBERT**, are the designated models for this task. These architectures are industry standards for natural language processing because their attention mechanisms process the bidirectional context of lexical tokens, capturing nuances that traditional bag-of-words models miss.

### Example Processing

When an email containing the text:
> *"Your account has been suspended. Click here immediately"*

is passed into this module, the Transformer:
1. Tokenizes the input
2. Processes the semantic weight of the suspension threat
3. Outputs a continuous probability score (e.g., `phishing_probability = 0.91`)

Training this module requires vast corpora of both **benign corporate communications** and **confirmed malicious lures**.

---

### Dataset 1: The Legitimate Baseline - The Enron Corpus

#### Overview

The primary advantage of the Enron corpus is its authentic representation of informal and semi-formal workplace communication, organizational discourse, and real-world linguistic behavior. Because genuine corporate datasets are rarely made public due to severe privacy constraints and non-disclosure agreements, Enron provides an irreplaceable foundation for the "Legitimate" (0) class label.   

However, the raw Enron data presents several structural anomalies that must be addressed before ingestion into a PyTorch-based Transformer pipeline:

Integrity and Formatting Issues: The initial release suffered from severe data corruption. Subsequent cleaning efforts by the CALO (Cognitive Assistant that Learns and Organizes) Project, SRI, and the Massachusetts Institute of Technology were required to stabilize the data format.   

Missing Payloads: Attachments were systematically stripped from the dataset, and highly sensitive emails were redacted due to requests from affected employees. This means Enron cannot be used for Module 4 (Attachments).   

Address Normalization: Invalid or unparseable email addresses were systematically converted to formats such as user@enron.com or no_address@enron.com.   

When utilizing Enron for BERT, developers typically rely on structured subsets. For example, the Hugging Face distribution of the Enron dataset provides a cleaned, tabular format (CSV) that accelerates the preprocessing pipeline using the Pandas library. This allows the raw text to be easily isolated and passed into the AutoTokenizer for BERT training.   

The Malicious Corpora: Nazario and PhishingPot
To balance the benign Enron data, the module requires a diverse collection of confirmed phishing attempts. The historical baseline for this is the Nazario Phishing Dataset. Distributed by monkey.org, the Nazario collection is historically significant, segmented into multiple .mbox files spanning over a decade of evolving phishing campaigns, with iterations ranging from early 2005 through 2025. Because the data is stored in raw .mbox formats, engineers must utilize Python's mailbox library to build custom parsers that extract the core body text for BERT training while separating and reserving the headers for Module 3.   

Relying solely on historical, static datasets introduces the risk of chronological bias and concept drift—the phenomenon where a model's accuracy degrades as attacker methodologies evolve. To counteract this, the PhishingPot dataset must be integrated. PhishingPot is a dynamic collection of real, active phishing samples collected via live honeypots. This repository provides a continuous influx of modern threats, ensuring the BERT model remains calibrated to contemporary semantic deception techniques. However, utilizing honeypot data requires stringent sanitization; researchers must ensure that any sensitive information or internal honeypot routing addresses are anonymized (e.g., replaced with phishing@pot) prior to tokenization.   

Advanced Aggregations and Synthetic Text Generation
The performance of transformer models is intrinsically linked to the scale and diversity of their training data. To overcome class imbalance, researchers frequently construct highly aggregated datasets. The MeAJOR (Merged email Assets from Joint Open-source Repositories) Corpus was developed precisely to address these limitations. Created at the Polytechnic of Porto (GECAD), MeAJOR provides 135,894 data samples drawn from TREC-05, TREC-06, TREC-07, Nazario, and Nigerian Fraud datasets, providing a unified, preprocessed corpus suitable for immediate deep learning application.   

Furthermore, as generative Artificial Intelligence democratizes the creation of highly persuasive text, traditional grammatical errors—once a hallmark of phishing—are vanishing. To future-proof the Content Analysis module, researchers must incorporate synthetically generated datasets. The dataset curated by FrancescoGreco97 introduces a multiclass structure featuring 4,000 emails, explicitly splitting the data between human-generated text (Nazario/Nigerian Fraud) and LLM-generated text (ChatGPT and the malicious WormGPT). Training a DistilBERT model on LLM-generated text forces the algorithm to analyze deeper semantic manipulation and structural intent, rather than relying on surface-level typographical anomalies.   

Module 2: URL Analysis
The second tier of the Stacking Ensemble operates independently of the email's semantic text, focusing exclusively on the Uniform Resource Locators (URLs) embedded within the message. Attackers heavily obfuscate URLs to bypass static signature blacklists. Therefore, the URL Analysis module utilizes lightweight, highly interpretable models—specifically Logistic Regression, Random Forest, or an independent XGBoost classifier—to extract statistical features from the URL string and calculate a probabilistic risk score, such as a url_risk of 0.84.

Feature Engineering for URL Assessment
The algorithmic evaluation of a URL does not require visiting the web page; it relies on lexical and host-based feature extraction. The following features represent the foundational inputs required for the Module 2 models:

Feature Variable	Data Type	Analytical Relevance and Rationale
url_length	Integer	Phishing URLs are frequently padded with excessive characters (e.g., url_length: 34) to hide the true target domain from the user's immediate view.
num_dots	Integer	Legitimate domains typically use one or two dots (e.g., www.example.com). An excessive number of dots indicates sub-domain manipulation to bypass filters.
num_hyphens	Integer	Attackers frequently use hyphens in typosquatting attacks to imitate brand names (e.g., paypal-login-security.com).
https_present	Binary	While increasingly common in phishing due to free SSL certificates, the absence of HTTPS on a purported financial link remains a critical risk indicator.
domain_age	Integer	Expressed in days or months, newly registered domains (e.g., domain_age: 2) are highly correlated with ephemeral, single-use phishing campaigns.
num_subdomains	Integer	Excessive subdomains attempt to mimic complex corporate routing or obfuscate the primary top-level domain.
ip_address_used	Binary	Legitimate entities rarely use raw IP addresses in consumer-facing URLs. A value of 1 heavily skews the probability toward malicious intent.
When an algorithm encounters an input such as http://paypal-login-security.com, the Python pipeline extracts these exact features into a dictionary format (e.g., {"url_length": 34, "num_dots": 2, "ip_used": 0, "domain_age": 2}) which is subsequently converted into a NumPy array for ingestion by Scikit-learn's Logistic Regression or Random Forest models.

Foundational Lexical Datasets: ISCX-URL2016 and PhiUSIIL
To train models to recognize these statistical deviations, massive datasets containing both benign and malicious links are required. The ISCX-URL2016 dataset, developed by the Canadian Institute for Cybersecurity, provides a rigorous framework for this analysis. It contains 57,000 URL samples intricately categorized into distinct classes, including 35,300 benign URLs collected from Alexa top websites, 12,000 Spam URLs, 10,000 active Phishing URLs from OpenPhish, and 11,500 Malware URLs. The research associated with this dataset establishes that analyzing the obfuscation techniques applied to the lexical structure is a highly efficient method for proactive detection before any web page payload is detonated.   

While ISCX-URL2016 offers strong categorical differentiation, the PhiUSIIL Phishing URL Dataset introduces a massive scale and advanced derived features. Donated to the UCI Machine Learning Repository in 2024, PhiUSIIL comprises 235,795 instances (134,850 legitimate and 100,945 phishing URLs) with a 54-feature schema. Rather than relying solely on basic character counts, PhiUSIIL incorporates complex derived metrics such as the CharContinuationRate (to detect algorithmically generated domains) and the TLDLegitimateProb (tracking the historical reputation of specific Top-Level Domains).   

The Login Page Imperative: PILU-90K
A critical mathematical vulnerability in traditional URL detection models is their reliance on generic homepages to represent the legitimate class. In a real-world scenario, users rarely receive emails directing them to a homepage; they receive links to specific, complex login portals that inherently feature long query strings and tracking tokens. Models trained solely on homepages inevitably produce high false-positive rates when evaluating legitimate login URLs.   

The Phishing Index Login URL (PILU-90K) dataset addresses this critical gap. It consists of 90,000 URLs: 60,000 legitimate URLs (importantly including both index pages and active login portals) and 30,000 phishing URLs. Research using PILU-90K demonstrated that training models on these specific URL variations drastically reduces false-positive rates, ensuring that the Random Forest model does not penalize legitimate, complex authentication links.   

Module 3: Header Analysis and Authentication Verification
The third module shifts the analytical focus from the payload and text to the cryptographic transit mechanisms of the email itself. Sophisticated threat actors frequently spoof legitimate domains to bypass the semantic checks in Module 1 and the lexical checks in Module 2. Module 3 evaluates the raw email headers to detect spoofing, routing anomalies, and failed cryptographic checks, utilizing a Random Forest model to output a header_risk score (e.g., 0.95).

Analyzing Cryptographic and Routing Features
Email headers contain specific transit and authentication metadata that must be parsed and converted into binary or categorical features for the Scikit-learn Random Forest classifier. The module must analyze the From:, Reply-To:, Return-Path:, and Received: parameters alongside three core authentication standards:

Sender Policy Framework (SPF): Verifies if the connecting IP address is officially authorized by the domain owner's DNS records. The feature extracted is typically a binary spf_pass (1 for pass, 0 for fail).   

DomainKeys Identified Mail (DKIM): Validates a cryptographic signature attached to the email, ensuring that the message content and headers were not tampered with in transit. This is extracted as dkim_pass.   

Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance (DMARC): A policy layer that dictates how receivers should handle emails that fail SPF or DKIM checks, enforcing strict domain alignment between the From: address and the authenticated domains.   

A simple passing grade for SPF or DKIM is insufficient to guarantee legitimacy; attackers often register their own domains and successfully pass SPF checks. The critical engineered feature here is sender_match (or DMARC alignment), which verifies that the domain authorized by SPF actually matches the domain displayed to the user in the From: field.   

Datasets for Header Parsing
Acquiring datasets with intact, realistic headers is notoriously difficult, as public datasets often strip routing information to anonymize senders. The Nazario dataset is an exception, as its .mbox format retains the raw header architecture, allowing researchers to build custom parsing scripts in Python to extract the Received hops and authentication fields.   

For a pre-engineered tabular solution, the "Spam Genuine Mail Contents Dataset" curated by Isuranga provides an optimal structure for Module 3. This dataset offers a balanced distribution of 50,000 legitimate and 50,000 spam emails and includes a wide range of metadata fields pre-extracted for machine learning ingestion, including explicit fields for SPF, DKIM, and DMARC authentication results, as well as the number of Received headers and originating IP metrics. Training a Random Forest model on this dataset allows the algorithm to map the non-linear decision boundaries between mismatched Return-Path variables, failed DMARC alignment, and malicious intent.   

Module 4: Attachment Analysis and Metadata Heuristics
Phishing campaigns frequently utilize emails as a mere delivery vehicle for malicious payloads. Module 4 focuses exclusively on evaluating attached files. Crucially, in a fast-acting Stacking Ensemble, this module does not dynamically detonate the malware in a sandbox environment; rather, it relies on static analysis and metadata heuristics to flag highly suspicious attachments using an XGBoost classifier, resulting in an attachment_risk score (e.g., 0.76).

Extraction Variables and Executable Threats
The module must systematically parse the attachment metadata to identify suspicious file extensions and hidden execution vectors. While simple executables (.exe, .scr) are easily flagged, threat actors continually adapt their obfuscation techniques to bypass standard gateway defenses. Phishing emails heavily rely on attached documents containing malicious Visual Basic for Applications (VBA) macros to initiate secondary downloads or execute PowerShell commands.   

The Python pipeline must extract the following features into a structured format:

File Extension Discrepancies: Identifying high-risk extensions such as .exe, .scr, .zip, .docm, and .xlsm. The pipeline must verify if the stated extension matches the true Multipurpose Internet Mail Extensions (MIME) type.   

Execution Flags: A binary feature (is_executable) indicating if the file has execution permissions or structural headers indicative of a binary application.   

Macro Presence: A binary flag (has_macro) indicating the presence of VBA scripts within Office document formats.   

Statistical Entropy: A continuous variable (e.g., entropy: 7.9) measuring the randomness of the file's binary data. High file entropy is a definitive mathematical indicator of packed, obfuscated, or encrypted malware attempting to hide its true signature from static analysis.   

Simulated Metadata Datasets
Because handling live malware poses extreme security risks for student projects or baseline research environments, relying on simulated metadata is the standard operating procedure for building the Attachment Analysis module. The "Simulated Email Dataset" created by Vincent Amonde specifically addresses this need. It provides simulated metadata for email attachments, reflecting realistic patterns of malicious communication—including entropy scores and extension mismatches—without the privacy concerns or systemic risks associated with actual file inspection.   

By passing these engineered dictionaries (e.g., {"has_macro": 1, "is_executable": 0, "entropy": 7.9}) into a Pandas DataFrame and subsequently into an XGBoost model, the algorithm can effectively learn the precise statistical thresholds where high-entropy files combined with macro presence overwhelmingly indicate a malicious payload.

Module 5: Psychological and Behavioral Profiling
The final independent module of the Stacking Ensemble recognizes that phishing is not merely a technical exploit; it is fundamentally an exploitation of human psychology. While Module 1 (BERT) evaluates the broad semantic meaning of the text, Module 5 isolates specific psychological levers—such as urgency, financial threats, and authority impersonation—to calculate a targeted behavioral risk score.

Psycholinguistic Features and Persuasion Dimensions
Social engineering relies on adversarial strategies to elicit immediate, irrational responses from end-users, bypassing their critical thinking faculties. The module must systematically scan the email body to extract specific, quantified features:   

Urgency score: The frequency and intensity of temporal demands (e.g., "URGENT!", "immediately").

Threat score: Language indicating negative consequences (e.g., "suspended", "terminated", "legal action").

Financial score: Language related to unexpected monetary transactions, invoice requests, or payroll anomalies.

Operational requests: Binary flags indicating the presence of password reset requests or unexpected multi-factor authentication prompts.

The most advanced dataset currently available for modeling these specific psychological triggers is the SpaPhish dataset. Although SpaPhish is a Spanish-native corpus, its architectural schema provides a brilliant mathematical blueprint for extracting behavioral features in any language. SpaPhish annotates emails across five specific psychological persuasion dimensions derived from Ana Ferreira's framework, including Authority, Social Proof, Liking/Deception, Commitment, and Distraction/Urgency.   

To replicate this in the Python environment, developers utilize NLP techniques—such as Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) models from Scikit-learn, combined with custom psycholinguistic lexicons—to parse datasets like Enron and Nazario. This process converts an input like "URGENT! Verify account immediately!" into a precise feature dictionary: {"urgency_score": 9, "threat_score": 7, "financial_score": 2}.

Regional Linguistics and the GenAI Threat
A critical blind spot in modern behavioral profiling is the assumption of a strictly English-language environment. Attackers frequently bypass standard keyword filters by utilizing regional languages. To ensure the behavioral module is robust on a global scale, it must be adapted to localized datasets. Repositories hosting classified news and text data across languages like Gujarati, Hindi, Marathi, and Telugu provide the necessary linguistic foundation to train NLP models in diverse regional deception tactics.   

Furthermore, the necessity for structured behavioral analysis is drastically increased by generative AI. LLMs are now utilized to generate highly personalized, contextually flawless spear-phishing emails that lack the traditional grammatical errors that Module 1 might catch. Training Module 5 on mixed datasets containing LLM-generated text ensures the system relies on underlying psychological metrics (the inherent threat or urgency score) rather than superficial textual anomalies, providing a critical safety net against AI-driven social engineering.   

Feature Fusion Layer and Meta-Learner Integration
Once the five independent modules are trained on their respective specialized datasets, their internal weights are frozen. They no longer operate as final binary classifiers; instead, they function as advanced, domain-specific feature extractors. The true power of the Stacking Ensemble is realized in the Feature Fusion Layer and the subsequent Meta-Learner.

Constructing the Secondary Dataset
When a new email enters the system, it is processed simultaneously by all five modules. Each module outputs its specialized probabilistic risk score (a continuous variable between 0.0 and 1.0). The Feature Fusion Layer is a simple programmatic step that aggregates these outputs into a single, one-dimensional array or vector: [bert_score, url_score, header_score, attachment_score, behavior_score].

For example, an email might yield the following array: [0.91, 0.84, 0.95, 0.76, 0.88].

To train the final Meta-Learner, an entirely new, secondary dataset must be generated. This is achieved by running a large, hold-out validation dataset (a mixed corpus of known legitimate and phishing emails that none of the base models were exposed to during their initial training) through the five modules. The output of this massive parallel processing is a tabular dataset—easily managed in a Pandas DataFrame—where the "features" are the five probability scores, and the "target" is the original binary ground truth label (1 for Phishing, 0 for Legitimate).

Instance ID	Mod 1: BERT	Mod 2: URL	Mod 3: Header	Mod 4: Attach	Mod 5: Behavior	Ground Truth Label
Email_001	0.91	0.84	0.95	0.76	0.88	1 (Phishing)
Email_002	0.12	0.05	0.10	0.01	0.05	0 (Legitimate)
Email_003	0.85	0.10	0.90	0.00	0.80	1 (Spoofed Phish)
Email_004	0.20	0.15	0.05	0.85	0.10	0 (Internal IT Update)
XGBoost Optimization Strategies
The designated algorithm for this final Meta-Learner is XGBoost. XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) excels in this apex position for several critical mathematical and structural reasons:

Non-Linear Feature Interaction: XGBoost naturally models the complex, non-linear relationships between the five independent modules. For example, consider Email_003 in the table above: it possesses a benign URL score (0.10) and no attachments (0.00), but yields a massive Header Risk score (0.90) and Content Risk score (0.85). A simple averaging ensemble might classify this as legitimate due to the low URL and attachment scores. XGBoost, however, constructs decision trees that learn that this specific permutation strongly indicates a highly sophisticated, spoofed email leveraging a clean redirect link.

Inherent Regularization: XGBoost features built-in L1 (Lasso) and L2 (Ridge) regularization. This mathematically penalizes overly complex models and prevents the meta-learner from overfitting to one dominant module. Without regularization, the ensemble might learn to rely entirely on the BERT score while ignoring the crucial nuances provided by the header or attachment analyses.

Tabular Optimization: As a gradient boosting framework, XGBoost is inherently optimized for the tabular, continuous probability distributions generated by the base models, making it far more efficient and accurate in this layer than attempting to use another deep learning neural network.

The Meta-Learner receives the fused array (e.g., [0.91, 0.84, 0.95, 0.76, 0.88]) and outputs the final, unified probability: 98.2% phishing, triggering the final system decision to quarantine the communication.

Training Process and Technology Stack Implementation
Implementing a five-tier Stacking Ensemble with an XGBoost meta-learner is a highly complex systems engineering challenge. It bridges the gap between basic machine learning experiments and the architecture of enterprise-grade or research-level cybersecurity systems. To execute this architecture successfully, developers must adhere to a strict, sequential training process utilizing a specialized Python technology stack.

The Technology Stack Ecosystem
The architecture relies on the seamless integration of several core Python libraries, each handling a specific mathematical or data processing role:

Python: The foundational programming language orchestrating the entire pipeline.

Pandas & NumPy: Pandas provides the DataFrame structures necessary to manipulate the massive tabular datasets (like Enron CSVs and the Feature Fusion layer). NumPy handles the high-performance array computations required for transforming the final probability arrays into a format ingestible by the machine learning algorithms.

PyTorch & Transformers (Hugging Face): PyTorch serves as the foundational tensor computation library, enabling the dynamic compilation of computational graphs required by the bidirectional transformer architecture. The Hugging Face Transformers library sits atop PyTorch, providing the pre-trained weights and tokenizers specifically required for the BERT and DistilBERT implementation in Module 1.

Scikit-learn: Provides the core classical machine learning algorithms—specifically RandomForestClassifier and LogisticRegression—used for the URL and Header modules. It also provides the vital Metrics functions (accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score) necessary to evaluate the individual module performance before fusion.

XGBoost: The highly optimized gradient boosting library utilized both as a standalone classifier for the Attachment metadata in Module 4 and as the apex Meta-Learner executing the final decision logic.

The Sequential Training Pipeline
Attempting to build, train, and hyperparameter-tune all five modules simultaneously will invariably result in catastrophic data leakage, memory exhaustion, and unresolvable debugging loops. The training process must follow a strict, isolated progression:

Step 1: Train BERT Separately (Module 1)
The raw email text from datasets like Enron and Nazario is isolated via Pandas. It is passed into the PyTorch/Transformer pipeline for tokenization and fine-tuning. The model is trained until it successfully outputs reliable content probabilities.

Step 2: Train URL Model Separately (Module 2)
The URL string data is processed to extract lexical features (url_length, num_dots, etc.) as defined by frameworks like PhiUSIIL. These features are structured into DataFrames and passed into Scikit-learn's Random Forest algorithm for isolated training.

Step 3: Train Header Model Separately (Module 3)
Header datasets are parsed to extract the routing and authentication binary values (spf_pass, dkim_pass). These structured categorical features are fed into a separate Scikit-learn Random Forest model.

Step 4: Train Attachment Model Separately (Module 4)
Simulated attachment metadata is processed to extract variables like entropy and has_macro. This highly specific numerical data is ingested by an independent XGBoost classifier.

Step 5: Prediction Collection and Feature Fusion
A pristine, unseen validation dataset is passed through all four (or five, including behavioral) trained modules simultaneously. The Python environment collects the predictions from each model (bert_prediction, url_prediction, header_prediction, attachment_prediction, behavior_prediction) and structures them into a new NumPy array/Pandas DataFrame.

Step 6: Train XGBoost Meta-Learner
The newly generated DataFrame of predictions serves as the input features for the final XGBoost model. The algorithm is trained to correlate these fused probabilities with the final ground-truth labels, resulting in the completed Stacking Ensemble.

Reality Check: A Progressive Implementation Roadmap
For researchers or students tackling a diploma-level or final-year B.Tech project, attempting to implement all five modules alongside a stacking ensemble in a single iteration is functionally unrealistic. The complexity of managing PyTorch tensors alongside Scikit-learn matrices and custom header parsers requires a phased approach. A highly practical progression path ensures a functional model is available early in the development lifecycle, which can then be systematically improved:

Baseline Generation: Begin by implementing only Module 1 (BERT) and Module 2 (URL Analysis). This establishes a core pipeline capable of analyzing the two most fundamental aspects of a phishing attack: the semantic lure and the malicious payload location.

Cryptographic Expansion: Once the baseline is stable, introduce Module 3 (Header Analysis). This requires shifting focus toward data engineering—specifically parsing raw .eml files to extract SPF and DKIM markers.

Heuristic Expansion: Add Module 4 (Attachment Analysis), utilizing the simulated metadata datasets to avoid handling live malware.

Psychological Expansion: Implement Module 5 (Behavioral Features), applying NLP rule-based heuristics to calculate urgency and financial threat scores.

Ensemble Construction: With all independent modules functional and yielding probability outputs, construct the Feature Fusion layer and build the final XGBoost Stacking Ensemble to aggregate the logic.

This progressive methodology transforms an overwhelming, monolithic engineering task into a series of highly manageable, modular data science objectives.

Synthesis and Architectural Implications
The architecture of a Stacking Ensemble reflects the uncompromising reality of modern cybersecurity: no single feature, heuristic, or algorithm can unilaterally verify the intent of sophisticated digital communication. Monolithic models are inherently vulnerable to highly specific evasion tactics; an attacker might craft perfectly benign text using an LLM, thereby bypassing a standalone BERT model entirely. By compartmentalizing the detection process into isolated semantic, lexical, cryptographic, payload, and psychological evaluations, the stacking architecture forces an attacker to perfectly spoof every single operational vector to achieve a successful system bypass—a statistically improbable feat.

The viability of this entire framework is inextricably bound to the rigor of data selection and feature engineering. Utilizing pristine corporate text (Enron), highly derived lexical obfuscation metrics (PhiUSIIL, PILU-90K), simulated payload entropy, and synthetically generated LLM lures guarantees that each isolated module is trained on the absolute bleeding edge of threat intelligence. By structurally fusing these specialized probabilistic assessments through an XGBoost meta-learner within a robust Python ecosystem, engineers can deploy a highly resilient defense mechanism capable of adapting to the rapid, asymmetrical evolution of global phishing campaigns.
